Why I dropped out of college at 19 — with no backup plan.

Hey, I'm Dhruv. I'm 19.

Let me tell you who I really am before I tell you what I did. I'm just a curious kid who has never been able to sit still when life offers a challenge — even the ones nobody asked me to take. I go looking for them. I always have.

I haven't sat inside a regular classroom since the 9th grade. For almost ten years, I played professional cricket instead. Ten years of early mornings, aching legs, and learning how to hold my nerve when everything was on the line. It was the best chapter of my life, and it taught me more about discipline and about myself than any textbook ever could. I walked away from cricket in June 2025. The why behind that is a whole other story, and it still hurts a little to tell, so we'll save it for another day.

Then one fine day, I joined Manipal Academy of Higher Education in Bengaluru for a Bachelor's in Commerce (Hons). And I was genuinely excited. A classroom, a campus, a normal life — it was brand new to me. I chose the course on purpose. I love finance. I love investing. This wasn't random.

My plan was clean. Finish the degree. Do an MBA from a good college. Land a role at an investment bank. And then, one day, build something of my own, create real brand value, become someone who mattered.

While a lot of my friends were still trying to figure out their next move, I already had my map drawn. And I was proud of that map.

The break that broke my map

College life was good. I made friends — some I'll carry for life, some I've already outgrown, and one quiet college crush that only a couple of people know about (yeah, we'll come back to that).

My attendance was a disaster. My grades sat at a comfortable average. But I finished my first semester, and then came a month-long break.

That one month rearranged everything I thought I wanted.

I couldn't sit idle, so I threw myself at everything. I tried content creation with a page called Burfi. I started a tiny marketing agency and shut it down a few weeks later. I did a sales internship. I met people who had nothing to do with college, people building real things in the real world.

And slowly, something inside me shifted. For the first time, I could see the world clearly — where the market was heading, what the job market actually looked like underneath the shiny placement stats, how much your connections quietly decide your future, and how badly you need to keep learning skills that match the times you're actually living in.

I walked back into my second semester as a different person than the one who left.

Everything felt slightly off now. Some friendships had grown closer, others had gone cold. The same crush, still there in the corner of my mind. And the dream itself had quietly changed shape: forget investment banking. I could see where that road was heading, and I didn't want to spend my one life betting on it. The new plan was simpler and scarier — build my own thing, create brand value, run startups, and drop out.

The word that gave me permission

Around this time, I grew close to my cousin brother, Vishnu Vijayan. We talked about life for hours, worked on a few projects together, and one day he handed me a phrase I didn't understand yet. He calls me chotu. He said, "Chotu, you're a high-agency guy."

I nodded like I got it. I didn't. So later that night, I Googled it.

High agency: the ability to shape your reality by taking personal ownership of your outcomes.

I read that line three times. Something in my chest went quiet and certain. He told me what he already believed about me — that I was meant to build, to lead, to matter. And then he did the thing everyone needs at least once in their life: he backed me. Figure it out, he said. And go.

Here's the part I can't skip over. It was the second month of my second semester. My attendance had crashed to 27%. And the truth is, I had a dream but no plan — no clear idea of what I'd actually do the morning after I walked out.

I decided to pitch it to my parents anyway.

Two nos and one very long night

I already knew how it would go. My dad might understand — he's the one who once told me to leave school after 9th grade and go all-in on cricket. My mom would shut it down before I finished the sentence. That was the script playing on loop in my head.

So I flipped the order. I sat my mom down first and laid out everything — the dream, the plan, all of it. And to my complete shock, she kind of agreed.

Then came my dad. I walked in full of confidence.

He said no.

Finish your degree. Then we'll see.

I went numb. And the hardest part wasn't the rejection — it was that he was right. I still couldn't look him in the eye and tell him exactly what I'd do once I left. He wasn't crushing my dream. He was protecting me from a version of it I hadn't built yet. And I knew it.

So instead of sulking, I got to work. I got serious on LinkedIn. And later that same week, everything cracked open — I landed an offer letter from Morphic, an AI company, with a real monthly salary. I won't share the number, but it was more than I'd have earned walking out of a Big 4 placement with a degree in hand.

Finally. A first step I could actually point to. I pitched it to my parents again, around late March 2026.

They said no. Again. And this time, my mom teamed up with my dad. Finish your degree. We'll see later.

I can't lie — that second no hit harder than the first. When the two people who love you most both say no, you start to wonder if you're the fool in the story.

The thirty minutes that ended the argument

The next day, I met a guy named Palak Zatakia, a growth lead at Morphic. We sat down at Araku Coffee in Indiranagar for thirty minutes and talked about my role, my life, my plans. Turns out he's a dropout too — and he's doing beautifully, touch wood.

So I asked him the question I really came to ask: "If you had the chance again, would you still drop out?"

He said yes. No pause. No flinch.

Then I asked, "Would you tell me to drop out?"

And he said something I've carried with me ever since:

"The credit and the blame should belong to the same person — you. Make your decisions, and stand solid behind them."

I thought about that line the entire day. Because he was right. I'd spent weeks asking everyone else to hand me permission — my mom, my dad, my cousin, a stranger over coffee. But the credit and the blame were always going to be mine anyway. So the decision might as well be mine too.

The next morning, I walked into college and applied to withdraw. And just like that, I dropped out of Manipal.

Then I went home and told my parents what I'd done. Not asked. Told. I made the call, and I stood behind it — trembling a little on the inside, but standing.

Where I am now

I'm writing this on the day it all feels real.

I've been at Morphic for five months. I've started a sales intelligence startup, Cnvrted, which I'm building shoulder-to-shoulder with Kailas Krsna and Sharan S. My days are packed with founders and creators, an inbox that never empties, and WhatsApp threads full of VCs and startup madness. It's chaos. I love it.

And my parents — the same two people who said no, twice — are now my biggest believers. My dad has quietly become the best mentor I could ever ask for. My mom has become my best friend. And Vishnu is still right there in my corner, exactly where he's always been.

I'm learning 10x more than I ever did in a classroom. Every single day is ups and downs, shipping things I once thought were out of my league.

(Still the same crush, by the way. Ha. Some things don't change.)

Strip it all back, and I'm just a 19-year-old kid walking toward everything he ever dreamed of — no map, no safety net, just conviction and the people who love him.

If I could hand you one thing from all of this, it would be this: you will make a million decisions in your life. Make them yours. Own the credit, own the blame, and stand solid behind every single one. That's the only lesson you'll ever really need.

Do I miss college? Yeah. Of course I do. Mostly the friends, and all the stupid, beautiful things we did together. I'll never pretend that part didn't matter.

But if you asked me to walk back into that classroom and choose again?

I'd make the exact same call.

And that's it.

Dhruv
